## Tension: DV × MR

Alignment
Both chapters converge on the same core strategic architecture with strong mutual reinforcement. They agree that sovereignty — domestic gateways, lawful intercept, data localization — is the primary market-access gate and Samsung's principal competitive wedge against U.S.-controlled LEO incumbents (evidence weight: strong in both, corroborated by specific regulatory actions like Vietnam's 4-gateway mandate and Singapore's Cybersecurity Act expansion). They agree the direct-to-consumer residential segment is structurally unattractive due to terminal affordability constraints ($349–$600+ hardware) and Starlink's >90% consumer LEO monopoly, and that the viable path runs through B2B2C and B2B wholesale to MNOs. Both identify the same beachhead geographies — Indonesia, Philippines, Saudi Arabia, UAE — and the same target buyer profile: Tier-1 MNOs and government ICT ministries holding veto power over market access. Both flag the identical adoption bottleneck: a 12-to-36-month regulatory and procurement gauntlet with large DMUs (5–18 stakeholders) and low deal survival rates (30–40%). Both call for the same immediate derisking action: securing LOIs from 2–5 Tier-1 MNOs before committing capital. And both chapters identify the same closing window — ITU Resolution 35 milestones and WRC-23/27 spectrum agendas creating a 2026–2029 action horizon.

Tension
1. Mobility segment prioritization diverges. Chapter B ranks Mobility (Maritime & Aviation) as the "Primary target" and highest-ARPU entry wedge ($12K–$300K/year). Chapter A barely mentions mobility, instead centering its "desperate segment" analysis on mid-market MNOs facing USO mandates and government ICT ministries. Chapter B simultaneously notes that Commodity Mobility is "oversaturated" with Starlink-driven price compression — an internal contradiction within Chapter B that Chapter A sidesteps entirely. The investment committee receives conflicting signals on whether mobility is the lead revenue anchor or an already-contested red ocean. Evidence weight for mobility as white space: thin.

2. Scale of the near-term revenue opportunity versus the >$10B target. Chapter B's constraint-based SOM is $174M–$256M in Years 1–3, scaling from a $30M Year 1 base. Chapter A frames a $66B–$109B annual regional opportunity cost and $437.5B in foregone GDP, but concedes zero anchor contracts, LOIs, or direct willingness-to-pay validation. Read together, there is a stark disconnect: the macro demand narrative from Chapter A implies a massive opportunity, while Chapter B's bottleneck-driven SOM suggests Samsung would capture less than 0.01% of its own SAM in Year 1. Neither chapter bridges this gap to the client's $10B annual revenue target within 10 years. Chapter B's SAM of $2.8B–$4.1B by 2030 itself falls far short; reaching $10B would require Samsung to capture multiples of its own serviceable market or for the SAM to expand dramatically — an assumption neither chapter validates.

3. MNO co-investment willingness is treated oppositely. Chapter A rates the risk that MNOs refuse to co-invest in gateway CapEx as "High probability / Critical severity" — its single most dangerous risk. Chapter B assumes the same co-investment model as the recommended path (form local JVs, build in-country gateways) but does not flag MNO refusal as a top risk; instead its risk table focuses on launch bottlenecks, terminal BOM costs, and price compression. This is not a factual contradiction but a framing tension: Chapter B's entry strategy depends on a condition Chapter A explicitly labels as likely to fail.

4. GCC competitive barriers. Chapter A cites the $5.1B UAE Yahsat mandate as evidence of sovereign demand, while Chapter B warns that state-backed incumbents in GCC markets present "high competitive barriers." These point in opposite directions: the same Yahsat budget that proves demand may also fund a competitor that blocks Samsung's entry.

Implication
The two chapters together validate the thesis that a sovereign-aligned LEO opportunity exists and that Samsung's corporate assets (semiconductor, RAN, Galaxy D2D) are well-matched to it. But they collectively expose a critical gap: no path from the validated $174M–$256M near-term SOM to the required $10B revenue target has been demonstrated. Downstream financial modeling must stress-test whether the SAM can realistically expand (via D2D maturation, mobility capture, and geographic expansion beyond four beachheads) fast enough within the 10-year horizon, or whether the $10B threshold is structurally unreachable with a ~3,500-satellite regional constellation.

The MNO co-investment question is the single highest-stakes unresolved dependency. Chapter A's validation experiments (conjoint analysis, gateway co-investment workshops) must be treated as hard prerequisites — not optional next steps — before any capital commitment. If MNOs refuse to co-fund gateways, Chapter A's pivot to a Samsung-funded OpEx model would fundamentally alter the capital structure and payback timeline that Chapter B's market sizing implicitly assumes.

Finally, the mobility segment requires an explicit verdict. The downstream analysis cannot simultaneously treat it as the "primary target" (Chapter B) and ignore it (Chapter A) without resolving whether Starlink's price compression has already closed the premium window.
