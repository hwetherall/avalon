The Reframe
The debate assumes that capturing the "sovereignty premium" requires Samsung to physically own and launch the orbital space layer. But the evidence reveals a lateral truth: the space layer (Layer 1) is a capital-incinerating oligopoly where raw capacity is commoditizing, while the ground and distribution layers (Layer 2/3) hold the actual regulatory control points. The non-obvious reality is that the regulatory moat isn't the satellite—it's the in-country gateway and the smartphone endpoint.

The Alternative Path
The Sovereign Multi-Orbit MVNO (Mobile Virtual Network Operator)

Samsung should outright abandon building a physical LEO constellation and instead build the Sovereign Network Architecture. Samsung will deploy physical, modular, in-country gateways integrated directly into the core networks of Tier-1 MNOs in Indonesia, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE.

Samsung then assumes the role of a hyper-MVNO: it purchases commoditized, raw orbital capacity from existing non-US, B2B-focused operators (e.g., Eutelsat OneWeb, SES MEO/GEO), routes that data securely through its local sovereign-compliant gateways, and bundles it with managed SD-WAN and cybersecurity orchestration.

Finally, Samsung leverages its massive Galaxy handset install base and 3GPP Release 19 NTN capabilities to offer Direct-to-Device (D2D) cellular access out-of-the-box, acting as the bridge between wholesale LEO capacity and MNO IMT spectrum. Samsung owns the customer SLA, the government compliance interface, and the end-user device, essentially turning LEO operators into interchangeable, dumb pipes supplying Samsung’s sovereign managed service.

Layer 1 Competition is Unviable: The market structure explicitly warns that "competing head-on in Layer 1 is unviable," and that success requires "exploiting fragmented Layer 2 via sovereign-neutral integration." (Evidence weight: strong).
Why It Might Be Better
This is the only path in the evidence set that solves the client's impossible financial guardrails. By abandoning the $5B+ constellation CapEx and the brutal $800M+ annual satellite replenishment costs, Samsung completely bypasses the Chinese/US launch bottleneck that is currently paralyzing Amazon Kuiper. A GSaaS (Ground-Segment-as-a-Service) and MVNO model dramatically alters the return profile, easily meeting the client's 11–13%+ IRR and 5–7 year payback mandates. It perfectly matches Samsung's true proprietary advantages (Samsung Networks RAN relationships and MX Galaxy devices) while passing the ruinous unit economics of space logistics onto third parties. It also scales to the $10B revenue target much faster by unlocking the $13.8B D2D market via software and partnerships, avoiding ITU licensing delays.

Why It Might Be Worse
Samsung sacrifices absolute control over its supply chain. By relying on third-party operators for raw capacity, Samsung is exposed if players like OneWeb or Telesat suffer financial distress, coverage gaps, or consolidation. Furthermore, if Starlink eventually shifts its strategy and capitulates to building localized gateways in Southeast Asia and the Middle East, Samsung would face a vertically integrated competitor with inherently lower latency and cost structures, leaving Samsung structurally squeezed in the middle.
