## Tension: MR × CA

Alignment
Both chapters converge on several foundational conclusions with strong evidence weight:

Sovereign-aligned B2B is the only viable entry path. Chapter A identifies 70–85% of ex-U.S. LEO revenue in enterprise/government/mobility segments and rates consumer residential as "Unattractive." Chapter B independently concludes Samsung must "align its category framing with the B2B and sovereign realities" and avoid replicating Starlink's B2C consumer motion. Both rate the consumer segment as a losing proposition against Starlink's 90%+ subscriber share and cost-per-bit advantage.

MNO partnerships are non-negotiable for D2D and mass-market scale. Both chapters identify MNOs as the gatekeepers controlling IMT spectrum and landing rights. Chapter A calls B2B2C wholesale "the only viable path to mass-market scale." Chapter B confirms D2D architecture "relies entirely on partnerships with terrestrial MNOs for legal spectrum access" and labels this a "borrowed strength" rather than a proprietary moat.

Launch access without vertical integration is a critical vulnerability. Chapter A rates launch bottlenecks as High probability/High severity. Chapter B escalates this to High probability/Critical severity, citing SpaceX's 84% commercial launch share and Amazon Kuiper's deployment paralysis from the same dependency. Both prescribe multi-vendor block-buy contracts with Blue Origin, ULA, and Arianespace as the primary mitigation.

Geopolitical fragmentation is simultaneously the primary threat and the primary opportunity. Both chapters identify sovereignty-driven regulatory tightening as creating defensible white space in SEA and MENA for a non-U.S., non-Chinese entrant. Both name Indonesia, KSA/UAE, and India as beachhead candidates. Both flag China and Russia as permanently closed.

Mobility segments command extreme premium pricing. Chapter A benchmarks maritime at $12K–$60K/year and aviation at $150K–$300K/year. Chapter B independently confirms aviation customers tolerate "$150,000 upfront hardware costs and $25,000 monthly service fees." Both recommend mobility as a primary target.

Tension
Severity of the launch bottleneck versus the proposed strategy's viability. Chapter A treats the launch constraint as a manageable risk, proposing multi-vendor block-buys and GEO/MEO partnerships for a hybrid Phase 1 rollout. Chapter B is far more pessimistic: it states Samsung's position is "highly fragile" because lacking proprietary launch vehicles "exposes the venture to the exact supply chain bottlenecks and margin compression currently crippling Amazon Kuiper and Eutelsat OneWeb." Chapter B explicitly notes OneWeb depends on SpaceX for launches—granting a direct competitor leverage over its cost structure—and that Amazon requested a 24-month FCC extension due to launch shortages. Chapter A's mitigation (block-buy contracts) does not address Chapter B's finding that the underlying problem is structural and unsurpassable, not contractual. Evidence weight for Chapter B's skepticism: strong, grounded in Kuiper's documented regulatory and deployment crisis.

Mobility segment: "Primary target" vs. "oversaturated." Chapter A labels Mobility (Maritime & Aviation) as "Highly Attractive" and recommends it as the "Primary target." Chapter B contradicts this by stating the "Commodity Mobility (Maritime & Aviation) segment is oversaturated and well-served, with Starlink triggering severe price compression," and notes Viasat holds 30–40% of aviation IFC with high hardware switching costs ($150K+ radome replacement). These findings pull in opposite directions on whether mobility is a realistic beachhead or an entrenched incumbency trap. Evidence weight: moderate on both sides—Chapter A relies on ARPU attractiveness while Chapter B relies on competitive entrenchment data.

Scale of obtainable opportunity vs. the $10B revenue target. Chapter A's SOM of $174M–$256M in years 1–3, scaling within a SAM of $2.8B–$4.1B by 2030, sits in direct tension with the client's primary success criterion of >$10B annual revenue within ~10 years. Chapter B does not provide its own revenue model but reinforces the difficulty by documenting that every non-SpaceX competitor is either stalled (Kuiper), subscale (OneWeb at <5% LEO share), or locked out (Guowang). Neither chapter demonstrates a credible path from a $256M Year-3 run rate to $10B by Year 10.

Implication
The downstream financial model must treat launch economics as a gating variable, not a line-item risk. Chapter B's evidence that the identical dependency is currently breaking Amazon's deployment timeline—despite $20B+ in committed capital—means Samsung's phased rollout timeline from Chapter A cannot be taken at face value without binding launch capacity commitments in hand.

The mobility segment requires immediate reconciliation: downstream analysis should test whether Samsung can capture share in mobility given Viasat's documented switching costs and Starlink's price compression, or whether the true entry wedge is exclusively MNO backhaul and sovereign networks.

Most critically, neither chapter bridges the gap between the near-term obtainable market ($174M–$256M) and the $10B revenue threshold. The downstream analysis must explicitly model the scaling mechanism—whether through D2D at scale, constellation expansion, or geographic rollout—that closes this 40x gap within the client's 10-year horizon, or flag the target as unreachable under the proposed strategy.
