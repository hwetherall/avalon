## How do you measure success:
Primary success criteria (highest priority):
•	Achieve >$10B in annual revenue within ~10 years
•	Reach high double-digit operating margins (high-20% range) at maturity 
•	Achieve cumulative cash-flow breakeven by year 7–8
•	Approx. IRR 11~13%+, 5~7 years payback. 

Secondary success criteria:
•	Establish Samsung as a credible, long-term global SatCom service provider, not merely a hardware supplier
•	Secure a defensible strategic position in non-U.S.-centric markets with regulatory or geopolitical barriers to U.S. incumbents
•	Build repeatable capabilities in satellite system design, operations, and service delivery
Trade-off guidance:
•	Slower global coverage is acceptable if early regional economics and regulatory positioning are strong
•	Higher upfront capex is acceptable if long-term strategic control and margin potential are preserved

## Anything else we should know before starting the analysis?


Samsung is exploring the creation of a satellite-based broadband communications business, conceptually similar to Starlink, with the ambition to deliver end-to-end satellite broadband services directly to consumers and enterprises rather than acting as a hardware or component supplier 
The initiative is being considered as Samsung accelerates its research and strategic exploration in the space industry, expanding beyond satellite manufacturing into full-service commercial space businesses 
Key contextual elements include:
•	The long-term vision is a Starlink-like business model, but with differentiation based on geography, regulatory positioning, and market focus, not direct competition in the U.S. market 
•	Initial target regions include Southeast Asia, the Middle East, and other non-U.S.-centric markets where broadband demand is unmet and where governments may prefer non-U.S.-controlled satellite infrastructure due to sovereignty or geopolitical concerns
•	Samsung has conducted preliminary internal analysis suggesting that a constellation of approximately 3,500 satellites could provide meaningful global coverage as an ultimate end state, materially smaller than Starlink’s constellation and potentially optimized for lower latitudes 
•	The business is expected to be highly capital intensive, with large upfront capex, gradual scale-up, and a long path to cash-flow breakeven 
•	Launch access, regulatory regimes (ITAR/EAR), and supply-chain choices are viewed as tightly coupled constraints that materially shape market access and feasibility 
This framing focuses exclusively on a civilian/commercial SatCom business, with defense or dual-use applications treated as out of initial scope to limit regulatory complexity

Inferred but testable assumptions:
